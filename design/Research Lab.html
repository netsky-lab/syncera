How does cream composition affect the skin? A cross-ingredient synthesis on permeation, irritation and photostability.

Key findings

4 verified
Permeation driver
Polysorbate 60 increased transepidermal delivery of lipophilic UV filters by 2.3–4.1× vs. non-surfactant controls.
Photostability
Ethylhexyl methoxycinnamate degrades ≈38% after 2 h UVA without photostabilizers; DHHB stabilizes it.
Irritation signal
Concentrations of Cetearyl Alcohol above 5.5% correlate with higher patch-test reactivity (n=14 studies).
Emulsion stability
C12-15 Alkyl Benzoate as secondary emollient keeps viscosity drift under 6% at 40 °C for 90 days.

Synthesis

auto-generated

Across the 275 primary sources indexed for this formulation, the strongest cross-paper signal concerns the interaction between non-ionic surfactants and lipophilic UV filters. Polysorbate 60, at the typical 1–3% emulsifier load, measurably enhances the stratum corneum uptake of ethylhexyl methoxycinnamate and C12-15 alkyl benzoate-dissolved actives[1,4,9]. This is not a safety red flag in itself, but it shifts the filter's residence profile from a surface-protective layer toward deeper skin compartments[2].

The evidence on photostability is unambiguous: ethylhexyl methoxycinnamate degrades rapidly under UVA in the absence of a stabilizer, losing roughly a third of its activity within two hours[11,12]. Reformulations pairing it with diethylamino hydroxybenzoyl hexyl benzoate (DHHB) retain >85% of initial SPF contribution at the same exposure[13].

On tolerability, cetearyl alcohol is broadly well-tolerated but a dose-response relationship emerges above the 5.5% threshold, with 14 of the 18 patch-test studies reporting elevated erythema scores at 6.0–8.5%[21,22,23].
